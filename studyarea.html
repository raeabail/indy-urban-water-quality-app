Study Area: Marion County, Indiana

Marion County is in the middle of Indiana and is home to the state’s capital, Indianapolis. It’s a mix of busy cities, quiet neighborhoods, farms, and parks. This makes it the perfect place to study how people and nature affect the water in rivers and streams.

Here’s what you should know:

  • Marion County is big, covering about 403 square miles.
  • It’s part of something called the Upper White River Watershed, which means all the rainwater that falls here eventually flows into the White River.
  • Some of the biggest rivers in the county are:
    • White River
    • Eagle Creek
    • Fall Creek

What Makes Marion County an Ideal Testbed?

Marion County has a little bit of everything:

  • Cities: like Indianapolis, where roads and buildings can cause rainwater to run off into rivers.
  • Farmlands: where fertilizers and chemicals can sometimes get into streams.
  • Natural areas: like forests and parks, where water stays clean.

By studying the rivers here, scientists can learn how to make the water better for everyone—fish, animals, and people!

What’s Happening to the Water?

Over the years, as more people moved in and more buildings were built, something called stormwater runoff has increased. When it rains, the water:

  • Picks up oil from roads, trash, and other yucky stuff.
  • Washes this into rivers and streams.
  • Hurts fish and plants that live in the water.

Why it matters to you:

  • Puddles near parking lots and sidewalks often carry oil and trash.
  • Fertilizer and lawn chemicals can wash into nearby creeks.
  • Road salt in winter ends up in streams when the snow melts.

But don’t worry! Scientists are working on ways to clean it up!
