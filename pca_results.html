Step 2: Standardize the Data

Each pollutant is measured in different units. Some are measured in mg/L, others in μg/L, some with small values, others much bigger.

To fairly compare them, we standardized all variables by calculating a z-score for every value.

This puts everything on the same scale, where each variable now has:

  • A mean of 0
  • A standard deviation of 1

Without this, bigger-numbered pollutants (like TDS) could dominate the results just because of their scale.

Additionally, data passed the Kaiser-Meyer-Olkin (KMO) and Bartlett’s tests, confirming it was suitable for PCA.

KMO Test

  • This test answers this question: "Is this data good for finding patterns?"
  • It tells you if your variables share enough in common to group them into broader trends.
  • A KMO value of 0.6 or greater indicates the data works well for PCA.

KMO value = 0.602

This score suggests the dataset is acceptable for pattern detection, which means PCA can uncover meaningful groupings.

Bartlett's Test

  • This test answers this question: "Are these variables connected at all?"
  • It tells you if there are real connections between your variables or if they’re all doing their own thing.
  • If the test result is significant (p < 0.05), it means your variables are related and good for PCA.

p = 0

A p-value of 0 indicates that the variables are strongly correlated, meaning PCA has a good chance of revealing meaningful patterns in the data.

Step 3: Correlation Matrix

Now we ask: How do the variables move together?

To find out, we created a correlation matrix: a table that compares each pair of variables.

  • A positive value means both variables tend to increase (or decrease) together
  • A negative value means one increases as the other decreases

This step lays the groundwork for uncovering patterns hidden across all variables.

Below, the correlation plot gives a visual summary of those relationships, where darker shades of either green (positive) or orange (negative) indicate stronger connections (Figure 1).

What does the correlation matrix reveal?

Now, we can really see the correlations! Significant ones are any where r > 0.60. In this case, there are three strongly positive correlations:

  • Arsenic & PO₄³⁻: r = 0.91 (very strong positive correlation)
  • SO₄²⁻ & Chloride: r = 0.63 (moderate positive correlation)
  • Cadmium & PO₄³⁻: r = 0.53 (moderate positive correlation)

All of these had p-values less than 0.05, which means the relationships are statistically significant.

Step 4: Extract Principal Components

Here’s where the real pattern-finding begins.

We used PCA to transform our dataset into a set of principal components: new variables that capture the biggest differences in the data. PCA does this by calculating:

  • Eigenvectors: The new directions or axes that maximize variance.
  • Eigenvalues: How much variance is explained along each direction.

The first component captures the most important variation. The second captures the next most. In many cases, only the first few components are needed to explain the majority of the variance in the dataset.

Scree Plots

We used two scree plots to decide how many components to keep. Figure 2 displays the eigenvalues from the PCA, where according to the Kaiser Criterion (Chatfield & Collin, 1980), only components with eigenvalues greater than 1 are retained. Figure 3 displays the principal components, showing how much of the total variance is explained by each component.

Step 5: Identify Dominant Parameters

Each principal component is essentially a new “axis” built from combinations of pollutants.

To understand what those axes really mean, we look at the loadings which are the pollutants that most strongly shape each component. Loadings answer two questions:

  • Which pollutants are driving the patterns?
  • How much each one matters?

Step 6: Cluster the Results

To explore how water quality varies across sampling locations at this site, we used two statistical tools:

  • K-means Clustering: An unsupervised technique that groups sites based on similar pollutant profiles.
  • Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA): A supervised technique that tests how well the groupings separate and identifies which pollutants matter most for that separation.

These tools allow us to turn complex PCA results into clear spatial patterns tied to actual sampling locations.

K-means Clustering

K-means clustering groups sampling sites based on similar water quality patterns. To figure out how many groups made the most sense, we used the silhouette method, which measures how well each site fits within its group compared to others.

Based on the graph below, the optimal number of clusters is 2, meaning the data naturally separates into two distinct groups (Figure 4).

Spatially, these clusters correspond to different parts of the watershed (Figure 5):

  • Upper Eagle Creek: Cluster 1 — likely influenced by suburban/residential runoff
  • Lower Eagle Creek: Cluster 2 — possibly impacted by downstream accumulation or urban discharges

Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA)

To validate the clustering, we applied Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA). This confirmed the separation between the two groups with:

  • 100% training accuracy
  • 93.5% cross-validation accuracy
  • Water quality patterns can be grouped into two distinct zones across Eagle Creek.
  • The clustering lined up with spatial differences (upstream vs. downstream) and may reflect land use, discharge points, or watershed dynamics.
  • Using both K-means and LDA helped confirm the structure of the data and identify which sites were most alike in pollutant profiles.

This final step helps us translate PCA’s statistical patterns into meaningful, place-based insights linking data back to geography and potential management strategies.

These results highlight how clustering can flag anomalous sites for further review, while confirming that Fall Creek’s water quality is generally uniform across sites.

For the Lower White River dataset, no pairs exceeded the r > 0.60 threshold for strong correlation. However, there are a few moderate associations worth noting:

  • SO₄ & TDS: r = 0.43 (moderate positive correlation), suggesting that dissolved solids in the river are partly driven by sulfate concentrations.
  • Chloride & TDS: r = 0.40 (moderate positive correlation), reflecting the contribution of chloride salts to overall dissolved solids.

Overall, the Lower White River matrix shows relatively weak to moderate correlations among pollutants, which suggests that multiple, independent sources are influencing water quality rather than a single dominant input.

Step 4: Extract Principal Components

Here’s where the real pattern-finding begins.

We used PCA to transform our dataset into a set of principal components: new variables that capture the biggest differences in the data. PCA does this by calculating:

  • Eigenvectors: The new directions or axes that maximize variance.
  • Eigenvalues: How much variance is explained along each direction.

The first component captures the most important variation. The second captures the next most. In many cases, only the first few components are needed to explain the majority of the variance in the dataset.

Step 5: Identify Dominant Parameters

Each principal component is essentially a new “axis” built from combinations of pollutants.

To understand what those axes really mean, we look at the loadings which are the pollutants that most strongly shape each component. Loadings answer two questions:

  • Which pollutants are driving the patterns?
  • How much each one matters?

We then used Varimax Rotation, a method that sharpens the picture by rotating the axes so pollutants cluster more neatly onto specific components. This makes interpretation easier and highlights the strongest contributors.

Step 6: Cluster the Results

To explore how water quality varies across sampling locations at this site, we used two statistical tools:

  • K-means Clustering: An unsupervised technique that groups sites based on similar pollutant profiles.
  • Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA): A supervised technique that tests how well the groupings separate and identifies which pollutants matter most for that separation.

These tools allow us to turn complex PCA results into clear spatial patterns tied to actual sampling locations.

K-means Clustering

K-means clustering groups sampling sites based on similar water quality patterns. To figure out how many groups made the most sense, we used the silhouette method, which measures how well each site fits within its group compared to others.

Based on the graph below, the optimal number of clusters is 4, meaning the data naturally separates into two distinct groups (Figure 14).

Spatially, these clusters correspond to different parts of the watershed (Figure 15):

  • Downtown Fall Creek: Cluster 1 — distinct in water quality, may reflect urban density or point-source discharges.
  • Uptown Fall Creek : Cluster 2 — share similar characteristics, likely reflecting general watershed patterns.

Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA)

To validate the clustering, we applied Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA). This confirmed the separation between the two groups with:

  • 100% training accuracy
  • 93.5% cross-validation accuracy
  • Water quality patterns across the Lower White River watershed are dominated by a single cluster, with smaller groups of sites forming secondary clusters.
  • Cluster 4: The dominant group, encompassing the majority of sites and representing the primary water quality pattern across the watershed.
  • Clusters 1 and 3: Smaller subsets of sites with localized variability in pollutant profiles.
  • Cluster 2: Did not dominate any site, suggesting it reflects sample-level variation rather than a distinct spatial zone.

These results indicate that the Lower White River is best characterized by one prevailing water quality regime, with a few locations showing distinct but localized differences.
