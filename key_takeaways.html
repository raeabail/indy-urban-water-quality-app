Key Takeaways

Over 24 years of monitoring, PCA and clustering tell a clear story for Eagle Creek, Fall Creek, and the Lower White River. Each site is governed by one prevailing water-quality regime with localized hotspots rather than distinct watershed zones. The most persistent signals involve chloride/sulfate/TDS (road salts and urban runoff), metals (legacy infrastructure and industrial pathways), and nitrogen–microbial dynamics (sewage and redox conditions).

Together, the results suggest current controls and natural buffering provide stability, while the biggest gains will come from salt-smart operations, legacy pipe/material replacement and pretreatment, riparian/green infrastructure in hotspots, and storm- and winter-timed monitoring. Long-term data and multivariate analysis are essential for setting policy and investment priorities.

Key takeaways at a glance

Dominant Pattern
One main water-quality regime per site

At all three waterways, most samples fall into a single dominant cluster. Smaller groups appear but typically reflect localized variability or short-lived anomalies rather than distinct watershed zones.

Salts & Metals
Chloride, sulfate, TDS, and metals shape the components

PCA consistently highlights Cl−, SO42−, TDS, and metals (Pb, Cr, Ni). These patterns point to road salts, urban runoff, and legacy industrial materials as recurring drivers.

Sewage & Nutrients
E. coli and ammonia flag local sewage influence

Signals from E. coli and NH3 indicate CSO/sewage impacts or reduced conditions, whereas PO4 aligns with fertilizer use and urban nutrient inputs in specific sub-catchments.

Site Stability
Relative uniformity within sites

Despite county-wide variability, site-level readings are fairly consistent with localized anomalies, suggesting current controls and natural buffering provide meaningful stability.
