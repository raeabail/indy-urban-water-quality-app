PCA Interpretations

Think of this as the “what it really means” page. We translate the rotated PCA components and clustering into clear, decision-ready narratives for Eagle Creek, Fall Creek, and the Lower White River. For each site, we outline:

  1. What We See
  2. Why We Think That
  3. Other Explanations
  4. What Now

We keep the focus on processes and potential management rather than statistics. A short cross-site synthesis, limits, and future directions appear at the end.

Additional notes:
  • Open a site below to read: what we see → why → other explanations → so what.
  • “+ / –” in PCA indicates axis direction only; magnitude carries the meaning.
  • Rotation is used for clarity; clustering is a light reality-check on patterns.

What We See

A primarily agricultural signal with elevated phosphate and periodic microbial indicators, consistent with runoff from fertilized fields and occasional fecal inputs. Sub-catchment differences likely reflect varying farm practices and buffer conditions.

Why We Think That
  • Rotated components group nutrients with trace elements, a pattern typical of fertilizer-driven runoff mobilizing residues.
  • Microbial pulses follow storm timing and drainage pathways connected to fields and ditches.
  • Clustering shows a generally coherent regime with upper vs. lower contrasts across land-use transitions.
Other Explanations
  • Failing septics or CSO events can mimic microbial signals; targeted bacterial source tracking would clarify.
  • Legacy materials or bank sediments may intermittently contribute trace elements during high flows.
What Now
  • Reduce nutrient pulses: riparian buffers, cover crops, edge-of-field practices in high-contribution sub-catchments.
  • Time monitoring: focus sampling after storm events and during fertilizer windows.
  • Fecal risk: coordinate with wastewater/septic programs where hotspots persist.

What We See

An urban footprint dominated by salts/ionic strength and legacy/heavy metals. Chloride tracks winter maintenance and concentrated runoff; metals reflect older infrastructure and historical industry.

Why We Think That
  • Rotated components align chloride/TDS/sulfate, typical of road salt and urban runoff.
  • Metal patterns match areas with older pipes, fill, and industrial corridors.
  • Clustering suggests one main regime with localized anomalies, not multiple stable groups.
Other Explanations
  • WWTP effluent can elevate ions; check timing against plant operations/flows.
  • Natural background is unlikely to explain the metals signal but should be screened.
What Now
  • Salt-smart operations: storage covers, calibrated spreaders, brining, and post-storm sweeping.
  • Legacy materials: prioritize lead service/pipe replacement zones and industrial pretreatment checks.
  • Green infrastructure: retrofit high-runoff blocks with infiltration practices and tree canopy.

What We See

A mixed regime: salts/ionic strength with industrial metals and reduced nitrogen. Signals reflect both agricultural and industrial pathways, with influences from legacy sediments.

Why We Think That
  • Ionic strength pairs with sulfate (and related markers), consistent with road salt and industrial/WWTP signatures.
  • Metals (e.g., chromium/nickel) indicate industrial or legacy inputs plus sediment interactions.
  • Clustering shows a dominant regime with smaller pockets of variability rather than distinct site regimes.
Other Explanations
  • Geogenic contributions to some metals are possible; bank erosion can expose legacy deposits.
  • Ammonia may reflect sewage influence or redox conditions in slower reaches.
What Now
  • Sediment & bank work: stabilize hotspots; assess legacy deposits near bends/outfalls.
  • Industrial controls: strengthen pretreatment and stormwater BMPs where metals recur.
  • Wetland/riparian restoration: boost retention and denitrification; add storm- and winter-timed monitoring.

Cross-site synthesis

Across sites, three recurring patterns emerge: salts and ionic strength, metals linked to legacy materials, and nitrogen–microbial dynamics. The prominence of these patterns shifts with land use: agricultural versus urban or industrial. Salts typically rise during winter maintenance and with urban runoff. Metals tend to track legacy infrastructure and industrial pathways. Nitrogen–microbial dynamics often reflect sewage influence and redox processes. Within each site, the overall regime is fairly uniform with localized anomalies, suggesting some effectiveness of existing controls and natural resilience.

Site Snapshots:
  • Eagle Creek: agriculture-linked nutrients with periodic microbial pulses
  • Fall Creek: urban salts with a metals signature from legacy infrastructure
  • Lower White River: mixed regime of salts + industrial metals + reduced nitrogen
  • Eagle Creek: PO₄, Cl⁻, Cr
  • Fall Creek: Cl⁻, Pb, E. coli (negative on VC3)
  • Lower White River: TDS, SO₄²⁻, Cr, Ni, Cl⁻, NH₃
Potential Management Priorities:
  • Target salts: optimize deicing & expand salt-smart storage and operations
  • Address metals: replace legacy materials & tighten pretreatment & stormwater BMPs
  • Reduce nutrient pulses: agricultural BMPs & riparian buffers & coordinate upstream
  • Sewage signals: continue CSO mitigation & add real-time E. coli/NH₃ checks

Notes on interpretation. Loadings and “dominant parameters” reflect the rotated PCA tables. Clustering references use silhouette-guided k and LDA verification. Patterns are interpreted alongside likely pathways: deicing salts, urban/industrial discharge, agriculture, and CSO/sewage influences.
